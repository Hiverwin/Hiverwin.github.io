I have been thinking about how to describe my relationship with mathematics.

For a long time, I would probably have answered in the most obvious way: it was a subject I loved, and a subject I happened to do well in. That answer is still true, but it feels incomplete now. Mathematics was never only a school subject to me. It shaped the way I learned to think, the kind of beauty I trusted, and even the kind of difficulty I was willing to stay with.

Maybe that is why I still like this phrase so much: *starting from mathematics, to anywhere*.

What I loved about mathematics was not only that it was hard. There are many hard things in the world. What felt special was the kind of clarity it demanded. It never seemed satisfied with impression, confidence, or approximate understanding. At some point, a definition had to become exact. A proof had to hold. A statement had to mean precisely what it claimed to mean. I think I was drawn very early to that feeling, that something difficult and abstract could suddenly become sharp, almost inevitable, if I stayed with it long enough.

And I did like staying with it. I liked the slowness of understanding. I liked the moment when confusion stopped feeling like a wall and started feeling like the shape of something I had not fully seen yet. I liked being forced to think carefully. I liked that beauty in mathematics often appeared quietly, after enough patience, rather than trying to impress me at first glance.

Looking back, I think mathematics taught me much more than content. It taught me certain habits that stayed with me even when I was no longer solving the same kinds of problems. It taught me to care about first principles, to ask what exactly something means, to notice where an argument breaks, and to feel uneasy around language that sounds sophisticated without actually saying very much. It also taught me that some questions only become understandable after you sit with them for a long time. That lesson has helped me far beyond mathematics itself.

There is also something emotional, almost personal, in the way I think about it now. Mathematics gave me a kind of trust in depth. It made me believe that difficult things are not always there to push us away. Sometimes they are inviting us to become more patient, more exact, and more honest. I think that changed me. It made me less afraid of complexity. It made me more willing to live with not understanding something immediately. It made me feel that confusion is not always failure; sometimes it is simply the beginning of real learning.

Even now, when I am working on things that look very different on the surface, I can still feel the influence of mathematics underneath. I notice it in the way I want to reduce a messy problem to its essential structure. I notice it in the way I search for the hidden assumptions inside a system. I notice it in the way I still care more about whether something truly makes sense than whether it merely sounds clever. In that way, mathematics never really stayed behind in one stage of life. It became part of my internal standard.

I do not mean that mathematics leads directly to every destination, or that it explains everything else I care about. I only mean that it gave me a beginning that still feels solid. It gave me a way of thinking that I can carry into many different places, even when the places themselves change. That is enough for me.

So when I say *starting from mathematics, to anywhere*, I am really talking about gratitude. Gratitude for the discipline itself, for the difficulty, for the elegance, and for the version of myself that it quietly helped shape.

And maybe that is the simplest way to say it: I still love mathematics, not only for what it is, but for what it taught me to become.
