I have been thinking about Lenny Rachitsky's view that AI is reshaping the product manager role in a fairly structural way, not just by adding a few new tools to the workflow.

What stayed with me is not the dramatic version of the claim, where PMs disappear overnight. It is the quieter version: AI compresses a lot of coordination-heavy, artifact-heavy work, which means the remaining value of a PM becomes more concentrated in judgment, direction, and taste.

That feels right to me.

## 1. A lot of PM work used to be translation work

Traditionally, product managers spent a meaningful amount of time translating between functions:

- turning user problems into specs,
- turning strategy into roadmaps,
- turning technical constraints into prioritization tradeoffs,
- turning decisions into documents that kept teams aligned.

This work is still real. But AI is starting to reduce the cost of some of its most visible outputs.

A decent model can already help generate:

- PRD drafts,
- user story breakdowns,
- meeting summaries,
- experiment ideas,
- basic competitive scans,
- first-pass UI copy,
- backlog decomposition.

That does not mean the work is solved. It means the document itself becomes less valuable as proof of ability. If everyone can produce a passable artifact faster, then the differentiator shifts elsewhere.

## 2. The job becomes less about producing artifacts and more about choosing direction

This is the part of Lenny's argument that I find most compelling.

If AI lowers the cost of execution support, then a stronger PM is not simply the person who writes cleaner docs or runs tighter process. A stronger PM is the person who knows:

- which problem is worth solving,
- which user signal actually matters,
- which tradeoff is acceptable,
- which feature should remain simple,
- which capability is technically impressive but strategically unimportant.

In other words, the center of gravity moves from documentation to judgment.

That also explains why the role starts to look more "CEO-like" at the edges. Not because PMs suddenly become mini-founders in a cliché sense, but because they are forced closer to the core questions:

- What matters now?
- Why this instead of something else?
- What are we optimizing for?
- What should the team ignore?

Those are not tool questions. They are direction questions.

## 3. PMs may need to become more builder-like

Another part I agree with is that AI raises the bar for product concreteness.

When prototype quality goes up and iteration cost goes down, it becomes easier to test product ideas directly. That changes expectations. A PM who can only talk in abstractions may start to feel slower than a PM who can pressure-test an idea through prompts, flows, mockups, or lightweight prototypes.

I do not think every PM needs to become a full engineer. I do think more PMs will need to become operationally builder-like:

- able to use AI tools to generate first versions,
- able to validate a workflow before asking a team to build the full thing,
- able to distinguish between a promising prototype and a misleading demo,
- able to move from concept to test quickly.

This shift feels especially strong in AI products, where the boundary between product design and system behavior is thinner than before.

## 4. Communication still matters, but not in the same way

One misunderstanding I sometimes see is the idea that if AI helps with writing and synthesis, communication becomes less important.

I think the opposite is true.

Communication remains essential, but it becomes less about formatting the perfect artifact and more about creating shared clarity. The real skill is not writing a polished document for its own sake. It is helping a team converge on:

- what the product is trying to do,
- what assumptions are still unproven,
- where the uncertainty sits,
- what the next decision depends on.

AI can help produce more words. It cannot automatically create better alignment.

That is why I think PM communication in the AI era becomes more selective and more strategic. The point is not to write more. The point is to reduce ambiguity faster.

## 5. Taste becomes more visible

One effect of AI that I find underrated is that it makes product taste easier to notice.

When many teams can move faster, generate more concepts, and ship rough versions more cheaply, the question becomes less "can you make something?" and more "can you make something worth keeping?"

That is where taste starts to show:

- what feels cluttered,
- what feels overbuilt,
- what should be automated and what should remain user-controlled,
- where explanation matters,
- where trust matters more than speed,
- where delight is useful and where it becomes noise.

This is especially true in AI products because models often make it easy to produce plausible surfaces. The harder part is deciding what kind of product experience should exist around that capability.

## 6. My own takeaway

What I take from Lenny's argument is not fear about the PM role shrinking into irrelevance. I take it as a sharpening of the role.

The low-leverage parts of PM work are becoming easier to automate or accelerate. That is real. But the high-leverage parts become more important precisely because everything around them moves faster.

For me, the question is no longer "Will AI replace PM work?"

The more useful question is:

**What kind of PM becomes more valuable when execution gets cheaper?**

My answer is:

- someone who frames problems well,
- someone who can hold product, user, and technical realities in the same picture,
- someone who can use AI to explore ideas instead of only discussing them,
- someone with enough judgment to know when a capability should become a product.

That version of the role feels more demanding, but also more interesting.

And honestly, that is part of why I want to work in AI product in the first place.
